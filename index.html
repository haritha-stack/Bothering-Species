Bothering Animal Species

Explore animals that frequently interact with human environments and activities.

High Threat

Black Bear

Ursus americanus

Large omnivorous mammals that often raid garbage cans, bird feeders, and occasionally enter homes in search of food. They can become dangerous when habituated to human food sources.

North America
Medium Threat

Coyote

Canis latrans

Highly adaptable predators that have expanded into urban areas. They may prey on small pets, raid garbage, and occasionally approach humans, especially if fed or habituated.

North America
Low Threat

Raccoon

Procyon lotor

Intelligent and dexterous mammals that excel at opening containers and accessing human food sources. They frequently raid garbage cans and may nest in attics or chimneys.

North America
High Threat

Wild Boar

Sus scrofa

Highly destructive to agricultural lands and natural habitats through their rooting behavior. They can be aggressive when cornered and reproduce rapidly, making them difficult to control.

Worldwide
Low Threat

Canadian Goose

Branta canadensis

These large birds often establish territories in parks, golf courses, and other grassy areas near water. They can be aggressive during nesting season and produce significant waste.

North America
Medium Threat

White-tailed Deer

Odocoileus virginianus

Adaptable herbivores that thrive in suburban environments. They damage gardens and landscaping, spread tick-borne diseases, and cause numerous vehicle collisions annually.

North America
Low Threat

European Starling

Sturnus vulgaris

Invasive birds that form large, noisy flocks. They compete with native cavity-nesting birds, damage crops, and create unsanitary conditions when roosting in large numbers on buildings.

North America, Europe
Medium Threat

Nutria

Myocastor coypus

Large, invasive rodents that damage wetlands, agricultural lands, and water management systems through their feeding and burrowing behaviors. They reproduce rapidly and are difficult to control.

North America, Europe

Prevention Tips & Recommendations

Learn how to minimize conflicts with wildlife while promoting coexistence.

Secure Your Trash

Use wildlife-resistant containers and store garbage in secure locations. Put trash out only on collection day, not the night before.

  • Use metal or heavy plastic containers with tight-fitting lids
  • Secure lids with bungee cords or weights if necessary
  • Store trash in garages or sheds until collection day

Wildlife-Resistant Landscaping

Choose plants that are less attractive to wildlife and use physical barriers to protect valuable plantings.

  • Use deer-resistant plant species in areas with high deer populations
  • Install fencing around gardens and valuable plants
  • Apply repellents to discourage browsing and digging

Seal Entry Points

Prevent wildlife from entering buildings by sealing potential access points and maintaining structures.

  • Inspect buildings for gaps and holes regularly
  • Install chimney caps and vent covers
  • Repair damaged soffits, fascia, and roof edges promptly

Remove Food Sources

Eliminate unintentional food sources that may attract wildlife to your property.

  • Use bird feeders designed to prevent access by squirrels and other mammals
  • Clean up fallen fruit from trees
  • Feed pets indoors and don't leave food outside

Deterrents & Hazing

Use humane deterrents to discourage wildlife from frequenting your property.

  • Install motion-activated lights or sprinklers
  • Use noise-making devices like air horns to scare away wildlife
  • Apply commercially available repellents according to label instructions

Know When to Call Professionals

Understand when a wildlife situation requires professional assistance.

  • Contact wildlife authorities for aggressive or unusual animal behavior
  • Hire licensed wildlife removal experts for animals in buildings
  • Consult with wildlife management specialists for persistent problems

Expert Insights

Perspectives from wildlife biologists and management specialists on human-wildlife conflicts.

Dr. Emily Richardson

Wildlife Biologist, University of Washington

"Many wildlife conflicts are preventable through simple changes in human behavior. Understanding animal motivations—usually food, shelter, or protecting young—helps us develop effective, humane solutions that benefit both people and wildlife."

Dr. Richardson's research focuses on urban wildlife adaptation and developing coexistence strategies for communities experiencing increased wildlife encounters.

Mark Stevenson

Wildlife Management Specialist, National Wildlife Service

"The most successful wildlife management approaches integrate scientific understanding with community involvement. When residents take ownership of local solutions, we see more sustainable outcomes and fewer conflicts over time."

With over 20 years of field experience, Stevenson has developed community-based wildlife management programs across North America that balance ecological needs with human safety concerns.

Dr. Sophia Chen

Urban Ecology Researcher, Cornell University

"As our cities expand into natural habitats, we're creating novel ecosystems where certain adaptable species thrive. These 'urban exploiters' aren't problematic by nature—they're simply responding to the resources and opportunities we unintentionally provide."

Dr. Chen studies how urbanization affects wildlife behavior and population dynamics, with particular focus on species that successfully adapt to human-dominated landscapes.

James Wilson

Agricultural Extension Specialist, State University

"Farmers are on the front lines of wildlife conflicts, but they're also some of our most innovative problem-solvers. The best agricultural practices incorporate wildlife considerations while protecting livelihoods, showing that coexistence is possible even in production landscapes."

Wilson works directly with farmers to develop and implement wildlife damage prevention strategies that are economically viable and environmentally responsible.
